What This Episode Is About

This is a long-form interview with Lam Weng Cheong, a professor of archaeology in the anthropology department at the Chinese University of Hong Kong, covering six core nodes in human evolutionary history: the origin of bipedalism, stone tool making, evidence of fire use, coexistence of multiple human species, the cost of the agricultural revolution, and the significance of archaeology in the age of AI.

The entire discussion is not a chronological historical narrative, but a dialogue that questions mechanisms: behind every "major human transition," what actually drove it, and what price did it exact? Lam Weng Cheong repeatedly emphasizes: evolution is not linear progress, but an adaptive choice with costs.

Plain Language Retelling

Humans and chimpanzees diverged from a common ancestor about 6 to 7 million years ago. For a long time, the gap between these two lineages was not as huge as it is today. Early hominins were actually more diverse than we imagine, with various species coexisting in different parts of the world, competing and interbreeding with each other.

Bipedalism is a key branching point. It didn't emerge for the simple reason of "standing up is more efficient"; behind it were complex evolutionary pressures, possibly related to climate change in Africa at the time, the reduction of trees, and the expansion of grasslands. Standing upright brought a series of structural consequences: it freed the hands, brains began to enlarge, heat dissipation capacity increased, allowing humans to chase prey for longer. But at the same time, the pelvic structure changed, making childbirth extremely dangerous—as babies' heads grew larger, the pelvic opening was narrowed by bipedalism, so birth required rotation and assistance from others, becoming a social event that "needed a midwife." Before the invention of antibiotics, maternal mortality rates were extremely high. This is a cost of bipedalism that persists to this day.

Tool making appeared earlier than many people think. The earliest cut marks found so far are about 3.3 million years old, left on animal bones, indicating that some stone-using behavior existed at that time. But the truly confirmed record of "systematic manufacture and use of stone tools" comes from Homo habilis about 2.4 million years ago. The ability to make tools implies a certain capacity for advance planning and spatial understanding—you have to first think about what you need, then find suitable stone, shape it into the desired form, and carry it away for later use.

Homo erectus appeared about 2 million years ago and was the first hominin species to truly leave Africa. Evidence of their fire use appears around 1 million years ago (debate centers on whether earlier evidence is reliable). Fire use was another transformative node: food could be heated, greatly increasing nutrient absorption efficiency, providing more energy for brain development. Persistence hunting also emerged during this period: after bipedalism, humans' efficiency in dissipating heat through skin sweat glands far exceeded that of quadrupedal animals, so chasing a prey animal for hours in hot weather would cause the animal to collapse from heatstroke while humans could keep running. This was a hunting strategy relying entirely on endurance, not strength or speed, and humans still retain this ability today.

The relationship between Neanderthals and Homo sapiens is far more complex than the narrative of "being wiped out by modern humans." The two interbred, and modern Asian and European genomes generally contain about 1-4% Neanderthal DNA. Denisovans are another hominin species that existed in eastern Asia, also leaving genetic traces, particularly high in some Southeast Asian populations. How these species were replaced—whether through competition, disease transmission, or simply differences in reproductive advantage—remains debated.

The emergence of language and abstract cognitive ability is difficult to pinpoint archaeologically because "speech" leaves no fossils. Scholars typically use burial practices as indirect evidence of cognitive symbolic ability—if a species buries its dead and even places grave goods in the tomb, it indicates an abstract understanding of "death" and an imagination of "some relationship continuing after death." Such evidence begins to appear around 60,000-70,000 years ago, with some earlier records being controversial.

The Agricultural Revolution began to take shape in West Asia around 12,000 years ago. But the term "Agricultural Revolution" can easily mislead people into thinking it was a rapid, active upgrade. In fact, from initial plant use to truly systematic domesticated agriculture may have taken several thousand years or more of gradual process. Lam Weng Cheong's assessment is that in many cases, the emergence of agriculture was not a rational active choice, but a forced result under specific environmental pressures (possibly climate instability, prey reduction, population increase) to sustain the basic survival of a larger population.

After agriculture, human living conditions worsened in many respects: food diversity decreased, relying mainly on a few crops, leading to nutritional monotony; dental disease increased because carbohydrates accumulated on tooth surfaces; overall physical condition visibly deteriorated in the archaeological record. A structural binding formed between humans and the land—if you have fields, you must guard them, losing the spatial freedom of the foraging era and the ability to diversify risk. In a foraging society, if you didn't catch anything one day, you could try again tomorrow; in an agricultural society, a single flood could destroy an entire year's harvest.

Gobekli Tepe is one of the most important archaeological discoveries in recent years. This large ritual complex, about 12,000 years old, was built earlier than or almost simultaneously with the known emergence of agriculture, challenging the mainstream narrative that "agricultural surplus precedes complex society." It shows that before agriculture was established, humans already possessed the social organizational capacity to coordinate large labor forces and complete large-scale construction projects. The assumption that "agriculture is a prerequisite for civilization" is, at least in some cases, invalid.

Finally, Lam Weng Cheong connects the understanding of ancient humans to today's AI era. His argument is not about how powerful AI will be, but rather: every major technological transition in human history comes with some irreversible cost as a prerequisite. Agriculture solved the food problem but bound humans to the land, reduced human flexibility in survival, and changed human health. AI provides unprecedented intelligent convenience, but what kind of cost might it impose on the structure of human relationships with knowledge and with each other? This is a question worth asking now, rather than looking back after it is complete.
