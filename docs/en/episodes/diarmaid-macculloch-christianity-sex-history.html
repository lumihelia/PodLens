What This Episode Is About

In this episode of Tyler Cowen's "Conversations with Tyler," historian Diarmaid MacCulloch — emeritus professor of the History of the Church at Oxford, author of Christianity: The First Three Thousand Years, Thomas Cranmer: A Life, and now Lower than the Angels: A History of Sex and Christianity — discusses his new book in a wide-ranging conversation covering two thousand years of Christianity's fraught relationship with gender, sex, and marriage.

MacCulloch's core historical argument is that Christianity's relationship with sex is far more complex and contradictory than either religious conservatives or secular critics acknowledge. Early Christianity contained a genuinely egalitarian impulse — most visibly in baptism replacing circumcision as the initiation rite (the former gender-neutral, the latter male-only), and in Paul's startling statement in 1 Corinthians 7:3 that "the husband's body belongs to his wife" equally as the wife's belongs to him. But the two thousand years that followed are largely a story of gradual retreat from that impulse.

The real rupture, MacCulloch argues, came in the twelfth century: the sacralization of the Eucharist required priestly purity, which required clerical celibacy — the first time in Christian history that all clergy were required to remain unmarried. The logic then cascades: if only laypeople have licit sex, marriage becomes foundational, and marriage requires the possibility of procreation. This twelfth-century invention is the root of what many mistake for timeless Christian teaching on sex. The Protestant Reformation then flipped it again: clerical marriage became mandatory, and the minister's family replaced the monk as the model of Christian life. MacCulloch also delivers a sharp critique of Foucault's history of sexuality, calling the claim that homosexuality was "invented" in 1869 "stupid and so French" — the historical record shows permanent equal-partner same-sex relationships long before the word existed. The conversation closes with MacCulloch's manifesto for the historical profession: "unsettling settled facts" to keep humanity sane.

Plain English Retelling

Most people, when they think about Christianity and sex, have one of two pictures in their heads. Religious conservatives see timeless natural law: marriage, family, procreation. Secular critics see a two-thousand-year project of sexual repression and gender hierarchy. Diarmaid MacCulloch's argument is that both pictures are anachronistic myths.

Start with what Christianity actually inherited. Judaism — Christianity's root — allowed polygyny (one man, multiple wives). Some Jewish communities practiced it within living memory. Monogamy didn't come from monotheism; it came from Greece and Rome, both polytheistic. Christianity adopted it not from scripture but from cultural strategy: you couldn't get the Greco-Roman world to listen to you if you practiced something that looked exotic to them.

Early Christianity then did something genuinely innovative: it replaced circumcision with baptism as the initiation rite. Circumcision is male-only; baptism anyone can receive. This quietly built gender equality into the foundation of the religion. And Paul — usually cast as the enemy of egalitarianism — wrote in 1 Corinthians 7:3 that not only does the wife's body belong to her husband, but "likewise the husband's body belongs to his wife." MacCulloch calls this "really extraordinary, and new" for the ancient world. Two thousand years of Christianity then spent their time quietly setting that text aside.

The twelfth century is where everything changed. The Western church was developing an increasingly elevated theology of the Eucharist — the bread and wine are literally transformed into Christ's body and blood by the act of a priest. Therefore, the priest must be as pure as possible. Purity rapidly slid into sexual purity. And so, for the first time in Christian history, all Western clergy were required to remain celibate — like monks. This had a strange logical consequence: the only people in Christian Europe now permitted to have licit sex were ordinary laypeople. So marriage suddenly became doctrinally important in a way it had never been, and it had to be potentially procreative. This is the historical origin of Catholic teaching against contraception — not ancient scripture, but a twelfth-century institutional cascade.

The Protestant Reformation then reversed it again, in a way MacCulloch — himself a minister's son — feels personally. Luther's most socially consequential act wasn't his theology; it was making clergy marry. Once clergy had wives and children, the minister's household became the template for how all Christians should live. The celibate monk, the organizing ideal of medieval Christianity, was simply abolished in most of Protestant Europe.

On Foucault: his claim that homosexuality was "invented" in 1869 just because the word was coined then is, MacCulloch says, "stupid and so French." Ancient records contain what look exactly like permanent, equal-partnership same-sex relationships — not the classical Greek model of an older tutor and a younger student (which is what Paul's obscure words arsenokoitai and malakoi probably describe), but people who built their lives together. The word was new in 1869. The reality was ancient.

His closing remark is his best: historians exist to unsettle settled facts. They don't cure cancer or go to the moon. What they do is stop lies from hardening into common sense — by going back to the sources, reading primary evidence, and showing people that what they thought was tradition is often invention, what they thought was nature is often policy.
