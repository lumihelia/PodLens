Principle, Life's Work, and Abiding Joy · Patrick O'Shaughnessy

Patrick O'Shaughnessy — founder of Invest Like the Best, Colossus, and Positive Sum — almost never sits on the receiving end of an interview. This conversation with David Senra is one of the few times he has. The result is less a podcast episode and more a mutual excavation: two people who have built their lives around understanding what drives others, now turning that same curiosity on themselves.

The Principle vs. the Goal

The most important concept in this conversation is one Patrick borrowed before he had his own: Bret Victor's "Inventing on Principle," a 2012 talk arguing that the most powerful creative lives are organized not around goals but around principles. The distinction sounds subtle but runs deep. A goal is a destination you want to reach. A principle is a belief about how the world should work — and when you see it violated, you feel a compulsion to correct it, and that correction is an act of service for others, not for yourself.

Victor's own principle: that digital creators should have instant feedback from their creation in real time, the way a painter sees each brushstroke as it happens. He built his entire career around eliminating the delay between thinking and seeing.

Patrick applied Victor's principle to his software company Canvas before he had articulated his own — and it worked. It took him another decade to put his own into words: when he sees enormous unrealized potential in a person or an idea, it is his obligation to surface it, to champion it, to help the world see what he sees. Whether through a podcast conversation, a Colossus profile, an investment check, or an introduction — it is the same act at different scales.

Growth Without Goals

At twenty-eight, pregnant with his first child, Patrick wrote an essay called "Growth Without Goals." The premise: if you are talented and you set goals, you will probably achieve them — but you will also give yourself blinders. You already know what's coming, which removes the surprise, and everything interesting gets filtered out before it can reach you.

Every genuinely consequential thing in Patrick's life came from the periphery. He didn't set out to build a media company. He loved reading and started an email list. The email list became a podcast. The podcast built relationships. The relationships led to investing, which led to Colossus profiles, which led to Positive Sum. None of this was on any goal list he could have written in advance. "And more exciting: it won't stop. The next ten years will produce a set of things I cannot imagine today."

The prerequisite for growth without goals is not passivity — it is having a principle that acts as a filter. Randomness without a principle is just drift. But principle plus openness produces something that feels almost like magic: the right opportunities appear, because you recognize them when they come from unexpected directions.

Dirty Fuel and Clean Fuel

David makes the observation that most of the founders they have both spent years studying were running on what he calls dirty fuel — fear, something to prove, something to escape. Dirty fuel works. It can produce enormous things. But it consumes the person who uses it.

The clearest illustration is Bruce Springsteen. He grew up with a traumatic, absent father, converted every ounce of that pain into obsessive work, became one of the most famous musicians on earth — and then fell into the deepest depression of his life at thirty-five. Because what he had built was not actually what he wanted. He wanted the capacity to love and be loved, and his childhood had specifically destroyed that capacity. Twenty-five years of therapy later, he got to the other side: "Life is more important than work. Work is part of life, not the whole of it."

LBJ is the counter-example. Same source material, same dirty fuel — but he never resolved it. He kept running it until it hollowed him out, using manipulation and aggression to the end.

The alternative is what the Upanishads call "abiding joy": a joy that does not run out. When you spend it, you receive more. This is distinct from money, power, or fame — each of which feels intensely good the first time and then traps you. Patrick read the key passage at twenty-six: "Those who feed the hungry protect me. Those who don't are consumed by me." It landed like a hammer. "The whole point of this existence is to help other people. That's it. That's the entire meaning of existence." He hasn't moved off that since.

Life's Work

Patrick defines the phrase precisely: "a lifelong quest to build something for others that expresses who you are." All three clauses are required. Thomas Jefferson's tombstone lists author of the Declaration of Independence and founder of the University of Virginia — not President of the United States. The presidency was his job. The other two were his life's work.

Very few people are actually doing their life's work. The phrase has been cheapened by overuse. Patrick screens for it in his investments: a founder who is genuinely doing their life's work will navigate adversity, boredom, and failure in fundamentally different ways than one who is executing a strategy.

The only maxim he repeats to his team: the reward for great work is more work. For someone doing their life's work, that is not a burden — it is the entire point.

How to Find the Best

Patrick's method for recruiting Jeremy Stern — who became editor-in-chief of Colossus — is a portable template. He wanted to build a profiles operation. He consumed as many long-form profiles as he could find. He found the best one (a Jeremy Stern piece on Palmer Luckey). He tracked down the author. He hired him.

Charlie Munger's version: "Find someone who is a great CEO and say, 'Come do this for us.'" The common logic: the work is the most objective signal of the talent. Ignore credentials, consume output, find the best, go get them.

The Eye of Sauron

Patrick's defining trait — the one his friends describe as "red on the color wheel" — is intensity of focus. When he is interested in something, he becomes almost impossibly effective at making it happen. The shadow side: the moment his attention shifts, it shifts completely. He can whiplash. People who depend on his sustained interest sometimes feel the ground disappear.

He also learned recently that he has aphantasia — he cannot visualize anything with his eyes closed. Just black. This turns out to correlate with almost no episodic memory: he rarely thinks about the past, and when he does, nothing comes as images. His resolution: "Simplify your life with rhythm and harmony" — a line from a very private mentor. Focus on fewer things. Stop being seduced by the new.

Relationships as the Only Scarce Asset

Patrick used to care very little about relationships. Now they are where he invests most deliberately. He has a list of ten to fifteen people for whom he would do anything, without explanation. Getting to that depth of trust requires roughly a hundred hours of real conversation — not networking, not pleasantries, but three-hour dinners where someone tells you something they have never told anyone else.

He keeps an internal conversation with Sam Hinckley running at all times — asking what Sam would think before almost every significant decision, at a frequency higher than any in-person interaction. "On my deathbed, I won't be thinking about five thousand podcast episodes. I will be thinking about these people."

Tim O'Shaughnessy

Patrick's first semester at Notre Dame: a stranger, no social network. His distant cousin Tim O'Shaughnessy made it his project to integrate Patrick into his social circle — going as far as calling friends when he couldn't attend a party himself: "I can't make it, but Patrick's coming. Take care of him."

Tim died young. But the act: Patrick met his wife Lauren at that first party, at the first bar, the first night, the first girl he spoke to. He met most of his closest friends the same year. "Entire lives are downstream of simple, quick acts of kindness. Without Tim, most of what I care about most deeply probably does not exist."

David Senra's grandfather left Cuba at thirty with no education, no English, and two jobs. That one decision rippled through the family tree. David is what Sam Hinckley calls the "founder of a family" — the person who makes the choice that changes the generational trajectory. The story of the father and the son, Patrick says, are often the same story.
