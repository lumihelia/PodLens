Company as Technology: The Obligation of Differentiation and the Founder's Craft · Tobi Lütke

Tobi Lütke has been building Shopify for twenty-one years. David Senra has spent ten years reading biographies of history's greatest founders. He says Lütke's name comes up repeatedly when he asks the founders he most admires who they most admire — the word other founders use is "singular." This conversation is what happens when someone who has studied everything ever written about how to build companies sits across from someone who has spent two decades actually doing it.

Company as Social Technology

Lütke opens with a claim that most people who work at companies have never thought about. A company, he says, is a social technology. Its fundamental function is to give you legal cover to do something that society otherwise won't tolerate: spending fourteen hours a day in total pursuit of one idea. Outside the frame of a company, that level of obsession looks like a problem. Inside it, it's called entrepreneurship.

The deeper function is to let you run what he calls "the counterfactual." You have a view of how the world should be that doesn't match how it is. A company lets you build that alternative and hold it up to the market. If the market agrees, it sends energy back to you in the form of money. If it disagrees, it tells you your counterfactual was wrong.

He ends the thought with the observation that the modern company is only about five hundred years old — and that if someone proposed the entire institution today, from first principles, it would sound insane. Thousands of people coordinating around one person's vision, exchanging their labor for promise of stock, agreeing to be governed by a hierarchy they didn't elect. The fact that it functions at all is remarkable. The fact that it occasionally produces something as good as Shopify is nearly miraculous.

The Saddest Day

Lütke has a benchmark for progress that he uses on himself continuously. He looks at old code — or old decisions, old frameworks, old strategies — and asks whether they embarrass him. If they do, he is learning. If they don't, something is wrong.

The saddest day of his career, he says, was the day he opened old code and found it impressive. That meant he had stopped progressing. He had spent years building a company instead of programming, and the gap had closed from the wrong direction.

He applies the same standard to the company. "All companies are terrible, including mine." This is not pessimism. It is the premise of twenty years of continuous improvement — the belief that the current version is the worst version that will ever exist, and therefore the most interesting version to work on.

Competitor versus Rival

There is a distinction Lütke draws early in the conversation that shapes how he thinks about the rest of it. He separates competitors from rivals, and treats them as nearly opposite forces.

A competitor makes a company reactive. He has watched organizations build entire internal systems around tracking what competitors are doing — the most active Slack channel becomes the competitive analysis channel, and the company gradually becomes a sophisticated follower. The ceiling is seven out of ten, because that is the score of the thing you are copying.

A rival does the opposite. A rival inspires you to be your best self. Agassi couldn't have been Agassi without Sampras. More striking: Michael Jordan, in The Last Dance, admits that he may have invented a slight to manufacture the fuel he needed. The rival doesn't even need to be real. What matters is the relationship to your own potential, not the other person.

The COVID Reckoning

After Shopify went public in 2015, Lütke made a mistake that took him fifteen years to fully recognize. He started "cosplaying a serious public-company CEO." Someone in a suit with a legal background, who delegates everything and trusts the report-up, who tells passionate people "I trust you to do the right thing" and then hears what they want him to hear.

The result was what you would expect. A team in the Toronto office had quietly decided that the supermarket industry was large and that Shopify should capture a percent of it. They built an entire product for running supermarkets. Nobody told Lütke. He discovered it when COVID arrived and he spent sixteen hours a day personally reviewing every project in the company.

He canceled sixty percent of the projects. Over the course of a year, he replaced every member of his executive team.

The mechanism he used to identify who should stay: crisis reveals people that predictions cannot. Before COVID, he says, he would have been entirely wrong about who would perform at a hundred and who would drop to zero. The single best predictor he found, in retrospect: had they ever started a company?

He went to a private Slack channel he kept for founders of acquired companies and asked for help. Every one of those founders who stepped up and ran something worked out. The observation that came out of this: large companies instinctively put their founders in "daycare" — skunkworks projects, peripheral initiatives, places where their refusal to accept bad answers can be contained rather than deployed. Lütke did the opposite. He put them in charge.

Shopify OS

Coming out of COVID, Lütke decided to treat the company itself as an engineering project. He opened a new repository on GitHub and started writing configuration files.

His diagnosis was that the company had accumulated layers of undecided things that nobody knew were undecided. A company with eight thousand people had five thousand five hundred distinct job titles. "Senior staff" ranked above director in some groups and below it in others. There was no coherent model. There were just accumulated path-dependent decisions that nobody had thought to challenge.

His solution borrowed a concept from software engineering he calls a desired state system. In React, when a user clicks a link, the library computes what the UI should look like, compares it to what currently exists, and makes the minimum number of changes to get from here to there. He wanted the same system for the company.

Working with a few engineers, he built a Python program that took configuration files as input — how many people should report to a manager, what title levels should exist, how compensation should be structured — and used a SAT solver to compute what the company should look like given those constraints. Then he held that model up to the actual company and found all the places where reality had drifted from intention.

The system, now called Shopify OS, changes the politics of resource allocation. When a sales leader comes asking for fifty new hires, the request goes into the model and immediately shows what has to give elsewhere — which engineering positions don't get filled, which product work stops. Nobody can accept a request while hiding its consequences. The decision is visible.

The Obligation of Differentiation

David Senra reads a line from James Dyson's autobiography during the conversation: "Differentiation and retention of total control. Make it different, even if it's worse."

Lütke says he completely agrees. His version of the argument: if you copy someone else's seven-out-of-ten solution, the ceiling is seven. You have no mastery over it. You can't improve what you don't understand from the inside. But if you build your own version from first principles — even if it starts at six — you own every part of it. You can iterate it past seven.

He calls this the most powerful thing in technology: the world belongs to people who move fast, iterate relentlessly, understand what is costly and what is unnecessary, and cut away the rest. The image he calls the most inspiring he has ever seen is the SpaceX Raptor engine evolution — a side-by-side grid showing the same engine getting progressively smaller, lighter, and simpler over years. Moving forward by subtraction. Almost no organization in industry can do this, he says. The ones that can are the ones worth studying.

The Brain as Retrospective Narrative Alignment Mechanism

Lütke reads neuroscience and has arrived at a conclusion he finds practically useful: the brain is not a record-keeper. It is a retrospective narrative alignment mechanism. Its job is to take the most salient version of your self-identity and reconcile your history with it. It rewards actions that are congruent with your identity and creates friction around actions that are dissonant.

This can be deliberately exploited. He was terrified of public speaking until he spent a week, ten minutes a day, writing: "I love public speaking." After a week, he did. Not as a placebo — he understood the mechanism. He was updating the reference identity, and the brain's alignment function did the rest.

He extends this to how he runs Shopify. He requires each of his executives to speak publicly every year about how their function at Shopify does things differently from the rest of the industry and why that's better. The stated reason is to force rigorous thinking about differentiation. The unstated mechanism is the same one behind affirmations: once you say it to him publicly, your brain will not let you be the person who said something untrue. The commitment becomes load-bearing for your identity.

He calls messages to his future self — notes written at the peak of insight, scheduled to arrive months later — "messages in a bottle." Spaced repetition for ideas he wants to keep.

Stewardship versus Ownership

Shopify does not allow engineers to use the word "ownership" about parts of the codebase. The permitted word is "stewardship." Once a pull request is merged, the code enters what Lütke calls the company's commons. It belongs to everyone. The person who wrote it contributed something and deserves credit — but cannot use it to block improvement.

This rule enables something unusual: he publicly trash-talks his own past work. Not to demoralize the people who built it, but to free them from deference to it. If the code is in the commons, there is nothing to protect. There is only the current state versus the better state, and the job is to close that gap.

He connects this to what the work actually is. A product is an abstraction — a name you put on a totality of details. From the inside, the product doesn't exist. What exists is thousands of small decisions, each of which either composes well or makes a bloody mess. "The way you do anything is how you do everything." This goes down to the floor plans of the office, the ambient noise levels in the hallways, whether the meeting rooms are on the window side or the interior side, whether the doors have proper affordances. He is allergic to Norman doors — doors designed so that people predictably do the wrong thing. The existence of a Norman door in a building is evidence that someone involved in the design process didn't understand their job.

The Corporate Raider Game

Lütke plays a game with himself every year. He imagines that Shopify has gone bankrupt and he has just bought it on a fire sale. The previous management was clearly crazy. He marches in on day one and asks: what would I do differently? The answer becomes his to-do list.

He teaches the same game to his teams when they are working on systems that aren't performing. Separate yourself from the prior work. Give yourself permission to be honest about what is wrong. Then fix it.

The cognitive function he is trying to defeat is the sunk cost fallacy, which he considers one of the most powerful forces working against good decision-making. His summary formulation: "The cheat code to always being right is to change your opinion every time you get better information." Being right is not a stable state — it is the output of continuous updating. The person who optimizes for apparent consistency over time optimizes for looking right rather than being right.

Going Public Early

Silicon Valley orthodoxy in 2015 told founders to stay private as long as possible. Lütke went public with eight hundred employees and a roughly one-and-a-half-billion dollar valuation, counter to what everyone was advising.

His framing: "We are not going public. We are creating a public version of Shopify."

The practical argument: accredited investor laws sequester growth exposure to people who already have capital. Public companies give ordinary people access to the appreciation of businesses they believe in. The counter-argument — losing control, dealing with quarterly calls — was real but overweighted. He learned things about his own business from having to explain it to investors. The currency for acquisitions was real. The name recognition in markets outside Silicon Valley was real.

The Formula One analogy: most rule books are read to understand how to comply with the rules. The F1 rulebook is read to find every possible advantage within the rules. Lütke applied this to the IPO process itself — discovering, for instance, that there was no rule requiring the roadshow video to be a CEO standing in front of slides. They shot a documentary instead.

StarCraft, AI Agents, and Year Twenty-One

Lütke learned more about resource management, decision-making under imperfect information, and attention as a scarce resource from playing StarCraft in his teens than from any business book. The game is played with incomplete information — you have to spend resources to scout what your opponent is building, then interpret what you find. Attacking your opponent's attention is often more valuable than attacking their base: if you overtax their ability to pay attention, they make mistakes. He never played the optimally prepared way. He made moves that weren't in any playbook — not because they were good, but because players who had prepared could not handle them. Fluid intelligence against crystallized intelligence.

Now, managing AI agents feels exactly like StarCraft. He runs six agents simultaneously. They send emails to each other, which he finds genuinely funny. He zooms in on the one doing the critical work, does some micro-management, checks the output of the critic agent that monitors all of them for drift. Same cognitive structure, different substrate.

He says the pace of change in early 2026 is unlike anything he has experienced. "If we had recorded this in week fifty-two of 2025, it would even be different." Three weeks changes what is possible.

He says: "If AI hadn't happened, I don't think I would still be running Shopify." The honest reason: his mission is to cause more entrepreneurship. If times are stable, there are better operators for a stable company. Lütke is most productive in conditions that are hard and interesting. Year twenty-one is the most interesting year in Shopify's history.

The 2014 Mission

In 2014, preparing for Shopify's internal company summit, Lütke was struggling with his own identity as a CEO who had left behind daily programming. He sat down to write a talk and realized something that had been true for his entire career without him naming it.

He loved engineering because as a child, it was how he could build things. What he actually loved was building things. More specifically: building things that let him share a particular experience — the moment of becoming an entrepreneur, the first sale, the feeling of a market saying yes to your counterfactual.

"I build products. Then I build companies that build products. And that causes more of this kind of thing."

Shopify is a tool for the thing he loves. The mission is to cause more entrepreneurship, which is why Shopify exists in Ottawa rather than San Francisco, why it goes out of its way to furnish its offices only from Shopify merchants, why it has run the same internal survey for years.

That survey found something important: roughly eighty percent of Shopify's customers had someone in their life who would reply to an entrepreneurial question within twenty-four hours. Lütke initially read this as evidence that his customers were lucky. Eventually he understood the causality ran the other way. The reason they are Shopify customers — the reason they tried — is because they had that person. Access to someone who demonstrates that starting is possible is one of the preconditions of starting. David Senra's podcast and Lütke's platform are both in the business of being that person, at scale, for people who don't otherwise have one.
