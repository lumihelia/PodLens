Adam Neumann is the co-founder of WeWork and the founder of Flow. He grew up in Israel, moved thirteen times before finishing school, spent five years in a kibbutz where his mother worked as the overnight doctor in exchange for housing, served as a Navy officer, and arrived in New York in 2001 four days after finishing his service. He built WeWork from a single floor in Brooklyn in 2010 to a presence in 130 cities across 50 countries by 2019, was removed as CEO in September 2019, and subsequently launched Flow — a residential real estate company backed by a16z. This conversation was recorded for Rick Rubin's podcast Tetragrammaton.

**The Kibbutz as Source Code**

The origin story Neumann returns to most is not WeWork. It is the kibbutz he lived in from approximately age eleven to sixteen.

A kibbutz is a communal settlement in which all residents earn the same, live in equivalent housing, and share responsibility for the community's infrastructure. The one Neumann's family joined was traditional: children slept communally on weekdays and returned to their parents only on weekends. His mother, a single parent who had recently divorced and was working nights as the camp's doctor in exchange for lodging, was frequently unavailable. On the nights when the household was volatile, there were always three neighbors who checked on him. No one judged. Everything was open. He was part of it.

He describes this not as nostalgia but as calibration. Every subsequent business — GreenDesk, WeWork, Flow — is an attempt to reproduce this experience at scale. The question he was trying to answer was not "how do I build a real estate company?" It was "how do I bring the kibbutz to the United States in the form of a business?"

**"Peanut Butter and Jelly" — Learning to Start Over**

When Neumann moved temporarily to Indianapolis at age eight for his mother's medical fellowship, he arrived speaking no English. His mother's instruction: say "peanut butter and jelly" in the cafeteria. For two months he said nothing else. Then one day he said "hot dog," and the language opened.

He tells this story as a structural pattern: each new community required a period of limited vocabulary, then a breakthrough, then fluency. He moved thirteen times. Thirteen times he entered a room knowing no one. He learned to find the kids who also knew no one, and to build something together.

This is the biographical root of WeWork's community thesis. It was not a product innovation. It was a personal theory about how belonging works, applied to commercial real estate.

**Rebecca and the First Kabbalah Class**

Neumann met Rebecca Paltrow through a mutual friend in New York around 2007. Their first meeting: she told him he was clearly broke and had cycled to their date. When they were a month into a relationship, she told him she wanted to be with someone who had a spiritual practice, and that she didn't think he had one or wanted one, so it probably wouldn't work out. She suggested he look into Kabbalah — specifically because he was Israeli and would resist anything that felt religious, and Kabbalah is mysticism rather than orthodoxy.

He went to prove to her that she couldn't reject him. The first lesson was a single sentence: when a challenge arises, before reacting, take a breath and say — "Pause. What a wonder." Then respond.

The next day he used it in a conflict with a difficult salesperson. She stopped. They had a real dialogue. They solved a problem they hadn't solved in months. He came back the next week hungry for more.

"I like things that work," he says. "Once it worked, I wanted more."

What followed was a decade of progressive practice: Kabbalah classes three times per week, eventually Shabbat observance, eventually morning meditation and prayer before waking the children. He describes the spiritual practice not as supplementary to his business life but as its operating system. His best business ideas, he says, come on Saturday mornings. He does not write them down — that is the rule.

**The Name**

After exiting GreenDesk, Neumann and his co-founder Miguel McKelvey spent six months trying to name the new company. They knew the business model. They could not find the name, and Neumann wouldn't start without one — by this point he believed in the power of words.

The name arrived at two in the morning, after both men had been working until exhaustion, ready to give up. Neumann's friend Andrew Finkelstein — the Hollywood agent who had introduced him to Rebecca — looked up with his eyes nearly rolled back and said: "Steve Jobs had it wrong. It's not I. It's We. We work. We give. We fly. We teach." Then he fainted.

Neumann woke up remembering it. He called McKelvey: "Trademark 'We Work.' Let's go."

He notes that the name came not from concentrated effort but from the moment of letting go. He connects this to the Biblical story of Joseph — from the pit to the prison to the palace, the breakthrough arrives only after the complete surrender of control.

**The Growth**

From 2010 to 2019, WeWork expanded from one floor in Manhattan to two buildings per day across 130 cities and 50 countries. Revenue grew from zero to $3.6 billion. The company entered China with a locally-funded entity at a $2 billion valuation, Latin America, Europe, and Southeast Asia. The brand became a verb: "the WeWork of this," "the WeWork of that."

Neumann attributes the early success to three things: the accuracy of the underlying thesis (people everywhere wanted to belong to something larger than themselves), the quality of the team that believed, and the nature of physical-world building — which imposed constraints that forced creativity. The first WeWork location was built on a tiny budget; he says they could never replicate how good it turned out to be, precisely because they were constrained.

Rebecca transformed the brand's language in her first ninety days: "Do what you love." "Create your life's work." "Build a world where people make a life, not just a living." Neumann had been operating with a logo of a man with a hammer breaking a Macintosh. She gave the company its soul.

**Masa and the $4.4 Billion**

In December 2016, Masayoshi Son walked into WeWork. Twelve minutes into the tour, he had seen enough. He asked Neumann to join him in his car uptown. Twenty-eight minutes later, a funding request that had begun at $300 million had been rewritten to $4.4 billion across WeWork and several regional joint ventures, and Neumann had signed.

Son's argument: the Vision Fund had $100 billion to deploy. A $300 million check was not a meaningful use of that resource. If Neumann believed in the business, why not go bigger?

Neumann's self-assessment in hindsight: "A Buddha sitting in that seat could have said no. I couldn't. Not the Adam that existed then."

He walked back to his headquarters on foot. He describes each step as the ego inflating: "It was under control until that day, and then it went above my spiritual threshold." The board meeting the night before had concluded with a plan to raise $300 million, turn the company profitable, and take it public at a modest valuation. Twenty-four hours later he was committed to a pace that required burning $1-2 billion per year.

He does not blame Son. "How could I be mad at someone who believed in me that completely? If he called me today needing anything, I would be on a plane." He locates the failure in himself: his spiritual practice was real, but not yet developed enough to hold this much weight.

**The Fall**

The $20 billion acquisition Son attempted in March 2018 was derailed by SoftBank's falling stock price in Japan. Son called Neumann from 15 minutes away — he happened to be nearby — to say he couldn't go through with it. Neumann says he should have called Son directly after that moment, laid out what had happened, and negotiated as a partner. Instead, the loudest voices around him were the bankers who wanted the IPO and told him Son was the enemy.

In 2019, Uber went public and its stock fell immediately. The public markets shifted from revenue multiples to profitability requirements overnight. WeWork needed nine months to turn the lever; they were mid-sprint. The S-1 was filed, the roadshow began badly, the valuation collapsed from a target of $47 billion to something undefendable, and Son called to say don't do it. The team around Neumann pushed forward anyway.

The IPO was pulled. The board asked him to step down. He stepped down. Nine minutes later, he received an email from the bank that had led the IPO process, the same bank with whom he held $435 million in personal debt: he was in breach of contract, and they were calling the loan. He had fifteen days to repay money he did not have and could not raise. The document was too sophisticated to have been drafted in nine minutes. It had been prepared in advance.

"I walked right into it," he says. "Terrible story. No — it's not a terrible story."

**The Floor**

He woke at 5 a.m. crying. He had not cried as an adult. In his own framework, nothing that happened to him as an adult had approached what he experienced as a child; therefore nothing warranted tears. This did.

Rebecca had never seen him cry. She listened to the situation, then said: "What have we actually lost? We have my mother's house in upstate New York. We take the kids, we homeschool for a while. If you want to go back into business, amazing. If you don't, we move to Costa Rica and surf. Either way — you have me and the kids. What are you crying about?"

And then: "Also, I've been meaning to tell you. When we met, you were broke. I find you much sexier broke than I do with money."

He says he smiled for the first time. He went downstairs to his three remaining staff — he had gone from 13,000 employees to three in weeks — and told them he didn't know how, but it was going to work out. They thought he had lost his mind.

His spiritual teacher called two days later and said: believing in the universe when everything is going well is not faith. That's common sense. Faith is when everything has collapsed and you can still choose to look up. If you can make that choice now, the version of you that comes out of this will be the one you were always supposed to be.

**Marcelo and the Settlement**

SoftBank's Marcelo Claure reached out for a meeting. He offered to pay Neumann's debt in full — not because it was owed, but because he objected to how the bank had handled the situation — in exchange for Neumann's controlling shares. The company would be funded, a new CEO would be installed, and the outstanding settlement owed to all shareholders and employees would be paid in the following year after the IPO.

The litigation that followed when SoftBank invoked force majeure after Corona hit in March 2020 led to the most consequential negotiation in the story. Rebecca, four days before giving birth by scheduled C-section, rejected the settlement Adam and the lawyers had negotiated. Her assessment: they were bluffing. Meet us in court.

Four hours later, Marcelo called directly — no lawyers. He said: "I want to apologize to both of you. You were ten years ahead of your time." The full payment was committed. At 4:30 a.m. on the morning of the C-section, the money cleared. At 9 a.m., Moses Neumann was born.

**Jared Leto and the Narrative**

Apple TV produced a dramatization of WeWork's rise and fall without interviewing Neumann. He sent a letter to Tim Cook arguing that a company committed to personal privacy could not simultaneously make dramatizations about real people without their consent. Cook's lawyers replied: you're a public figure, you will lose, we're moving forward.

He eventually agreed to meet Jared Leto, who was both starring and producing. Leto arrived at Neumann's house, put his hands on his face, and began speaking in what he described as Neumann's accent. Neumann, unfamiliar with method actors, found the whole thing slightly outrageous.

At the end of the meeting, walking Leto out, Leto said: "This was supposed to be a takedown. I know — I read the script. I wish we had met before. But I know who you are now, and I'm going to play you the right way. Like a rockstar."

The show aired. The day after, the inbound began: entrepreneurs, potential partners, future employees. People who watched it — including people who had never met Neumann — largely read him and Rebecca as the heroes.

**Marc Andreessen**

Shortly after Neumann stepped down, Andreessen called for the first time. Neumann missed it. A Wall Street Journal article described the Neumanns buying over a billion dollars of real estate in South Florida; Andreessen called again. The third call connected.

Their first exchange: Neumann began describing what had happened in terms that had absorbed some of the media's framing. Andreessen interrupted: "You're still in that phase — the one where you believe what other people are saying about you and forget what actually happened."

Six months of deepening conversation followed. Andreessen eventually came to Malibu to hear Neumann's thesis for Flow. After three hours on WeWork's lessons, Andreessen said the same thing he'd said on the phone: "We know what we think happened. Let's talk about the future of living."

The investment structure Andreessen proposed: Neumann would contribute $350 million in real estate equity. Andreessen Horowitz would contribute $350 million in cash. Complete alignment: when you buy, we buy; when you sell, we sell. "You were never actually misaligned with your previous investors. But let's make a rule so there's no ambiguity."

Andreessen's framing of Flow: "You're not building a better apartment complex. You're solving loneliness and ownership. If you can actually do that, you have a world-changing business. I'm here to build the big one, not the middle one."

**Flow**

The problem Neumann identifies: residential real estate technology is built on legacy databases that don't put the resident first. Every current platform is a database with AI layered on top; Flow is attempting to rebuild the data architecture from the ground up, starting with the resident as the organizing unit.

Their CTO came from WhatsApp, where he had architected systems for billions of users. Ben Horowitz chose him not for his technical track record but for a specific trait: when someone says something stupid in a meeting, he can't hide it on his face. "In a small tech team, one bad apple destroys everything. You always manage to the lowest common denominator. If the CTO's face tells people they're not excellent, they'll leave and find another job."

The Brickell building in Miami has 632 units. The market standard for filling a building this size is eighteen months. Neumann expects six. The driver is not advertising — it is a farmers market on what was a construction site, three music events per week, a running club, a walking club, and yoga classes, all staffed by residents who were given the tools to organize them.

On the outer wall of the building: "What if it all goes right?"

Rebecca wrote it. It has been viewed 22 million times.

**Shabbat and the Quiet Revolution**

Young people are going back to church. Bible sales are increasing. Something is happening.

Neumann's explanation: the more technology connects us digitally, the more isolated we feel in person. The more isolated we feel, the more we crave genuine connection. Genuine connection never comes from buying another car or another house. It comes from inside.

He recommends the experiment to everyone: put your phone in a jar for twenty-four hours. For the first three hours, notice how many times your hand reaches for your pocket. That is evidence of addiction. Make it through to the next morning. The ideas that come on the other side are different in quality from the ones that come when the phone is always present.

He keeps Shabbat. His best ideas come on Saturday mornings. He doesn't write them down.
